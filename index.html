What a day feels like

Most people don't think about what their body is doing to keep them upright. They stand up from a chair and walk to the kitchen without their heart racing, without their vision dimming, without a quiet negotiation happening between their brain and their blood vessels. Their body does thousands of invisible things per hour — adjusting blood pressure, redirecting blood flow, regulating temperature — and none of it costs them anything they'd notice.

Elyssa's body does all of those same things. It just costs her almost everything she has.

Morning

A healthy person sleeps 7–8 hours, spends about 20% of that in deep sleep — the phase where the body physically repairs itself — and wakes up near full capacity. Healthy Garmin users with good sleep routinely start the day at 85–100 on the Body Battery scale.

Elyssa sleeps nearly 10 hours. Despite all that time, she only gets about an hour of deep sleep — roughly half the minimum normal amount. Her brain spends a third of the night in vivid REM dreaming, far more than typical, while being shortchanged on the phase that actually restores her body. A healthy person's body recovers at about 12–14 energy points per hour of sleep. Elyssa's recovers at 6.9. She sleeps 30% longer and gets half the return. That's why she needs almost 10 hours to reach a morning level of 75 that a healthy person reaches in 7.

On her worst days, she wakes up in single digits. The day is over before it starts.

Standing up

When a healthy person stands, gravity pulls blood into the legs. Within a second — before they could even form a conscious thought about it — the brain detects a tiny pressure drop, fires a signal through small nerve fibers to the blood vessels in the legs, and those vessels squeeze tight. Blood pushes back upward. Pressure holds steady. The person doesn't feel a thing.

Elyssa's blood vessels don't squeeze properly. Blood falls into her legs and stays there. So her heart does the only thing it can — it beats faster and harder to try to push blood upward on its own. A healthy person's heart rate might rise 18 beats per minute from sleeping to standing. Elyssa's rises 37. Both of them end up around 90 beats per minute during the day — but the healthy person's heart climbed from 72 to get there. Elyssa's climbed from 57. Double the distance. Double the work. Every morning. Just to reach the same starting line.

The long drain

From the moment she's upright, Elyssa starts losing energy. And this is where her day diverges most from a typical person's.

A healthy person's energy curve has texture. It dips when they exert themselves and rises when they sit down, eat lunch, or relax for a few minutes. Healthy Garmin users routinely see their energy climb by 5 to 30 points during a quiet afternoon — watching TV, sitting at a desk, reading on the couch. Some describe their energy "staying almost full" through an entire day of desk work. On rest days, a healthy person might only lose 10–30 points across the whole day.

Elyssa's curve is a straight line down. On nearly two-thirds of her days, her energy drops from morning to night without a single upward bump. No recovery from sitting. No recharge after lunch. No bounce-back from resting on the couch. She loses about 5 energy points every waking hour, and here is the finding that stands out most in her data: it doesn't matter what she does. Whether she walks 11,000 steps or takes 95, the drain rate is almost identical. She loses 62 points on a rest day and 66 on an active day.

A healthy person who walks 11,000 steps drains significantly more than one who rests. Elyssa drains the same either way. Her body is spending nearly all of its energy on something invisible — the constant cardiovascular effort of keeping blood flowing to her brain against a system that won't cooperate. The walking barely registers on top of that baseline cost.

Afternoon

By noon, Elyssa's energy is at 45 out of 100. A healthy person at noon might be at 60. The gap has already opened.

By 3 PM she's at 29. The healthy person: 47. By this point in the day, Elyssa has entered the zone where language starts to slip. Reading becomes harder. Finding words takes effort. She can still think in images, still process spatial information — but the verbal system, which is the most energy-hungry thing her brain does, is running on fumes. This isn't a learning disability. It's an underfueled brain rationing its resources. On good days, speaking is easier. On bad days, it's the first thing to go.

By 6 PM, she's at 18. The healthy person is still at 39, and their body has already started shifting into evening recovery mode — their energy is beginning to tick back upward while they're still awake, sitting on the couch after dinner. Elyssa's body doesn't do this. Her system doesn't downshift into recovery while she's conscious. She just keeps draining.

Evening

By 9 PM, Elyssa is at 13 out of 100. Essentially empty. The healthy person, at the end of their most exhausting day of the week, might hit 20. Elyssa hits 13 on a normal day. On Tuesdays — her hardest day — she's at 10.

Then she sleeps. For ten hours. And claws her way back to 75.

And does it again.

But not every day is the same

That description is an average day. The reality is that her days cycle on a monthly rhythm that reshapes everything. During follicular phase — the week or two after her period ends — her body recovers better overnight. She wakes up at 82. Her heart rate stays lower. She crashes only 4% of the time. This is her best window, and it's when she feels closest to functional.

During ovulatory phase, the system breaks down. Her body's overnight recovery is impaired by the hormonal shift. She wakes up at 67 instead of 82 — starting 15 points behind before she's even stood up. Her heart rate at 3 PM hits 97.5 instead of 87. She crashes 17% of the time. The three worst crashes in the entire dataset all happened during ovulatory phase.

And on day 1 of her period — when blood loss is heaviest — she crashes half the time. The single worst day in five months of data was November 11: she went from 91 to 21 overnight, on the exact day her period started. She went to bed feeling fine and woke up in crisis.

This means she doesn't just have good days and bad days. She has good weeks and bad weeks, on a monthly clock, and many of the "random" crashes that seem unpredictable from outside are actually predictable once you know where she is in her cycle.

What the data says about all of this

These aren't impressions. They come from 140 days of continuous wearable monitoring, compared against 796 days of data from a healthy Garmin user and benchmarked against global averages from millions of users and an academic study of 781 individuals.

Her morning energy looks normal on paper — 75 versus the global average of 71. But that number hides what it costs: she sleeps 30% longer at half the recovery efficiency. Her daily energy drain of 67 points is 37% higher than the global average of 49. Her drain rate of 4.7 points per hour is 74% faster than our reference individual's 2.7. And on 64% of her days, her energy goes in only one direction — down — with no daytime recovery at all. The reference individual gets daytime recharge on 90% of days.

She's not tired because she does too much. She's not tired because she doesn't try hard enough. She's tired because her body spends nearly all of its energy on the invisible work of staying upright and perfused, and there's almost nothing left over for living.

Daily action plan

These are the things that help, based on 140 days of data. Print this page. None require a doctor's visit. All can start today.

DAILY Exercise — 15 to 20 minutes, every morning

Her muscles physically pump blood back to her heart and brain. Without this, her veins can’t do it alone. The day after exercise, her resting heart rate drops 6 bpm and her autonomic system runs calmer for 24 hours. This isn’t fitness training — it’s mechanical circulatory support.

How: Start with the floor workout (starts lying down, never requires standing). Once the home gym is set up, add light barbell work. On bad days when her body won’t move, even 4 minutes of ankle pumps and glute bridges while lying flat counts. Consistency matters more than intensity. Never push to max heart rate — the data shows a run at HR 169 during a vulnerable phase caused a multi-day crash.

When it matters most: During ovulatory phase (roughly days 15–21 of her cycle). This is when her system is most vulnerable and the exercise buffer is the difference between a hard week and a crash cascade.

DAILY Salt and electrolytes — 3 to 5 grams of sodium per day

More sodium means more blood volume. More blood volume means more blood available to pump upward to her brain. The standard POTS recommendation is 3–5g sodium/day — roughly double what’s recommended for healthy people. This isn’t unhealthy for her; it’s compensating for a system that can’t hold onto fluid properly.

How: Use quality electrolyte mixes with high sodium and low sugar — LMNT (1000mg sodium/packet), Normalyte (clinical-grade, designed for POTS), or Liquid IV. Avoid Gatorade (too much sugar, not enough sodium). Salt food generously. Some people add ¼ tsp salt to a glass of water.

When: First thing in the morning before getting vertical. Before co-op. Before any extended standing. Front-load — don’t wait until she feels bad.

DAILY Water — 2 to 3 liters minimum

Water expands blood volume, but only when paired with electrolytes. Water alone actually dilutes sodium and can make things worse. Always drink with salt or an electrolyte mix.

DAILY Lie flat for 15–20 minutes midday

Not sitting — sitting still pools blood in the legs (her zero-step heart rate averages 83 bpm while seated/standing). Lying flat removes gravity from the equation entirely. Legs elevated is even better. On Tuesdays, this is critical between co-op and the evening commitment.

WEEKLY Tuesday protocol

Tuesday is her worst day by every metric (BB 66.6, 4 crashes, HR 72.4). Co-op alone registers as a HIIT workout on her Garmin.

Before co-op: Exercise (even 10 minutes), electrolytes, large glass of salted water. Eat a salty breakfast.

During co-op: Sit whenever possible. Avoid prolonged standing — her heart rate hits 102 bpm while motionless at co-op vs 86 on Fridays.

Between co-op and evening: Lie flat for 15–20 minutes. More electrolytes. This window is the best chance to prevent Wednesday from being a crash day.

MONTHLY Track the cycle and plan around it

Her menstrual cycle is the second-biggest driver of crashes after exercise status. Ovulatory phase (days 15–21) has a 17% crash rate vs 4% in follicular. Period start day crashes 50% of the time.

Ovulatory week (days 15–21): Don’t skip exercise. Increase electrolytes. Plan lighter days if possible. This is the vulnerability window.

Day before expected period: Pre-load fluids and electrolytes. Plan nothing demanding. Accept the crash may come and structure the day for it.

Follicular phase (days 8–14): This is her best window. BB starts at 82, crash rate is 4%. If there’s something hard to schedule, put it here.

MEDICAL Iron supplementation

Get ferritin and CBC drawn now. Given her heavy menstrual bleeding and celiac disease (which impairs gut absorption), IV iron may be more effective than oral supplements. If oral: take with vitamin C on an empty stomach, away from calcium, dairy, and gluten-free grains. Recheck levels every 3–6 months given ongoing blood loss.

CONSIDER Compression garments

Waist-high compression stockings reduce venous pooling in the legs. Given her light touch hypersensitivity, these may or may not be tolerable — firm compression may be OK since she tolerates firm pressure but not light touch. Worth trying, especially on co-op days.

What not to do

Don’t rest more on bad days. The data shows rest costs more energy per stress point than movement (2.0 BB/stress vs 1.63). Sitting still with a heart rate of 83 is harder on her body than gentle walking.

Don’t skip exercise because she feels bad. She needs it more on bad days, not less. The exercise buffer is what prevents bad phases from becoming crash cascades.

Don’t push to maximum effort. A run at HR 169 during a hormonal transition caused a multi-day crash. She doesn’t need intensity. She needs consistency.

Don’t ignore the calendar. Crashes aren’t random. Once you know her cycle phase, most of them are predictable.

What to investigate

These are the medical steps worth pursuing. The daily management items are in the Daily Plan tab. These are the questions to bring to doctors.

Check her iron

A simple blood draw for ferritin and a CBC. The menstrual data now shows her body crashes 50% of the time on day 1 of her period — the day blood loss is heaviest. November 11, the worst crash in five months of data, was exactly day 1: BB plummeted from 91 to 21 overnight. If she's been losing significant blood monthly and not absorbing iron well due to celiac, her body may be running critically low on the raw materials it needs to carry oxygen and maintain blood volume. If ferritin is low, IV iron infusions (which bypass the gut entirely) can replenish stores in one or two sessions.

Talk to someone about the bleeding

Her menstrual flow — filling a Diva Cup every two hours, bleeding through extra heavy pads — is significantly heavier than typical. The data now confirms that her menstrual cycle drives crash risk more than almost any other factor: ovulatory phase produces 4× the crash rate of follicular phase. Every month of heavy blood loss directly undermines the blood volume her body is already struggling to maintain.

Consider seeing a geneticist

Her combination of chorioretinal coloboma, congenital heart condition, and lifelong autonomic dysfunction — especially with her daughter also having coloboma more extensively — suggests a genetic thread. A geneticist can evaluate whether they share an underlying condition, informing care for both.

Ask about small fiber neuropathy testing

A skin punch biopsy is a simple, in-office test that checks whether the small nerve fibers responsible for blood vessel control are intact. This is a conversation to have with a neurologist familiar with dysautonomia.

None of these steps require her to be a different person. She has been managing an extraordinarily demanding body her entire life. The goal is to make that work a little easier.

The bigger picture

POTS (lifelong), congenital heart condition, chorioretinal coloboma, celiac disease, chronic anemia, heavy menstrual bleeding, light touch hypersensitivity, medication sensitivity, language difficulty, anxiety, and depression. None of these exist in isolation.

The congenital foundation

Her coloboma affects the back of her right eye where retina and choroid sit — structures built by neural crest cells during embryonic development. The same cells build parts of the heart and the autonomic nerves controlling blood vessels. Two of those systems have structural differences. The third (autonomic nerves) may also have developed differently.

Her daughter also has coloboma — more extensively, in both the retina and iris (visibly apparent). This familial pattern strongly suggests a genetic basis and raises important questions for the daughter's cardiac and autonomic health.

The autoimmune layer

Celiac disease generates inflammation that can damage small nerve fibers — the exact fibers that tell blood vessels to squeeze. This may explain why symptoms worsened at 21 and again in her late 20s: autoimmune damage on a system with limited margin.

Light touch

She cannot tolerate light touch but handles firm pressure. These use different nerve fiber types. Light touch uses the same small fibers that control blood vessel constriction. A skin punch biopsy could test whether these fibers are damaged.

Blood loss

Heavy bleeding reduces blood volume monthly. Anemia means less oxygen per unit of blood. Celiac may prevent iron absorption. Every month is a setback her body may not fully recover from before the next one.

Language and cognition

She has significant difficulty with reading, writing, and speaking, but strong visual-spatial abilities. This fluctuates with her condition — on good days, speaking is better. Language processing is one of the brain's most energy-intensive tasks. When the brain is underfueled, language degrades first.

Anxiety and mood

Some anxiety may be her body's chemical attempt to maintain blood pressure (adrenaline). But chronic illness is also a legitimate source of psychological distress. It is likely both. Iron deficiency, poor sleep, and chronic illness are all independently associated with depression.

Definitions

Terms used throughout this document. These are explained in plain language, not clinical definitions.

Body Battery

A Garmin metric that estimates energy reserves on a 0–100 scale, calculated from heart rate variability, stress, and sleep data. It’s not measuring calories or blood sugar — it’s measuring how much capacity the autonomic nervous system has available. Think of it like a phone battery: 100 means fully charged, 5 means the phone is about to shut off. A healthy person typically wakes up at 85–100 and ends the day around 30–40. Elyssa averages 75 in the morning and 13 by evening.

Crash

A day where Elyssa’s Body Battery starts the day below 50 — meaning she never reached even half capacity at any point in the day. On crash days, her body has so little reserve that basic functioning becomes extremely difficult. Crashes aren’t just “tired days” — they’re days where the system has fundamentally failed to recharge overnight. She had 15 crash days in 140 days of tracking. Every crash maps to a combination of menstrual cycle phase, activity load, and exercise status.

Drain rate

How fast Body Battery drops per hour during waking time. Elyssa’s averages 4.7 points per hour regardless of what she does. A healthy person’s varies from 2–3/hr on rest days to 5–6/hr on active days. The fact that hers is flat — the same whether she walks 11,000 steps or 95 — means her energy is being consumed by something other than physical activity: the constant cardiovascular effort of maintaining blood pressure.

Daytime recharge

A period during waking hours where Body Battery actually goes up — meaning the body shifted into recovery mode while still awake. Healthy people experience this on ~90% of days (sitting on the couch after lunch, relaxing in the afternoon). Elyssa gets zero daytime recharge on 64% of her days. Her system doesn’t downshift into recovery while she’s conscious.

POTS (Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome)

A condition where the body can’t properly regulate blood flow when upright. Normally, when you stand, your blood vessels squeeze to push blood back up to the brain. In POTS, they don’t squeeze properly, so blood pools in the legs and the heart has to beat much faster to compensate. Elyssa’s heart rate jumps 37 bpm from sleeping to standing (a healthy person: 18 bpm). She’s had this her entire life.

HR swing

The difference between sleeping heart rate and daytime heart rate. Elyssa’s is 37 bpm (sleeping 57, daytime ~92). A healthy comparison individual’s is 18 bpm. This swing represents how hard her heart has to work just to be upright — it’s the cardiovascular cost of POTS made visible.

Menstrual cycle phases

Menstrual (days 1–7): Period. Active bleeding. Day 1 is the highest-risk single day — Elyssa crashes 50% of the time.
Follicular (days 8–14): Her best phase. Body recharges well overnight. Morning BB averages 85. Crash rate 4.3%.
Ovulatory (days 15–21): Her worst phase. Overnight recovery is impaired. Morning BB averages 72 — starting 13 points behind follicular. Crash rate 17.1%. The three worst crashes in the dataset were all ovulatory.
Luteal (days 22+): Mixed. HR runs highest in the mornings. Crash rate 8%.

Cascade

Imagine a bank account where she deposits a fixed amount each night (sleep) and withdraws all day (being alive). Normally, the deposit covers the withdrawal and she breaks even. But if she has a particularly hard day — say, co-op during ovulatory phase — she overdrafts. The problem is that when she starts the next day already in the red, her body can’t recover as efficiently that night either. So the next deposit is smaller. Which means the next day starts even lower. Which means the next night’s recovery is even worse. Each day digs the hole deeper.

This is a cascade: a self-reinforcing decline where one bad day makes the next day worse, which makes the day after that worse still. It doesn’t stop on its own. It stops when something breaks the cycle — a better hormonal phase arrives, she gets a genuinely restful day with no demands, or exercise re-establishes her vascular baseline. Without intervention, cascades can last over a week. The worst one in her data ran from Feb 23 to Mar 9: 11 days, 6 crashes, spanning three cycle phases, and it only ended when her body finally stabilized on cycle day 3 of the next period.

Exercise buffer

The protective effect of daily exercise on Elyssa’s system. When she exercises, her skeletal muscles physically pump blood back to her heart (the “muscle pump”), doing the job her blood vessels can’t. This lowers her next-day resting heart rate by 6 bpm and reduces crash risk from 31.6% to 7.4%. It’s not fitness training — it’s mechanical circulatory support. Without it, vulnerable cycle phases produce cascading crashes.

Co-op

A Tuesday homeschool co-operative that Elyssa attends with her children, starting January 27, 2026. Her Garmin classified co-op as a HIIT workout because her heart rate averaged 123–136 bpm with spikes to 185–199 — just from standing and managing children. This single misclassification demonstrates the invisible cost of POTS: her body at co-op is working as hard as a healthy person doing high-intensity interval training.

Resting heart rate (RHR)

The lowest heart rate recorded during sleep. Elyssa’s averages 51 bpm (range 40–67). This is actually low, which is significant: it means her POTS is likely neuropathic or hypovolemic (nerve or blood volume related), not hyperadrenergic (adrenaline-driven). The type matters for treatment.

Deep sleep

The sleep phase where the body physically repairs itself — tissue growth, immune function, cellular restoration. Normal is 15–25% of sleep time (about 70–120 minutes). Elyssa gets 9.9% (about 64 minutes). Half of her nights have deep sleep below 10%. This means her body gets less physical repair time despite sleeping nearly 10 hours.
